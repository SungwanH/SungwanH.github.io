Research

  • Green Industrial Policies and Energy Transition in the Globalized Economy - Job market paper
    • Abstract: The transition to renewable energy relies on the trade of renewable energy equipment (REE), such as solar panels and wind turbines. In this context, I examine the dynamic impacts of the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) on global carbon emissions and welfare by analyzing two green industrial policy instruments: a subsidy on renewable energy consumption (RE subsidy) and a subsidy on renewable energy equipment production (REE subsidy). I develop a dynamic trade model that incorporates tradable REE where countries accumulate REE as capital, and the productivity of equipment is endogenously determined through learning-by-doing. In the model, an RE subsidy has a static impact by increasing the relative price of fossil fuels and a dynamic impact by reinforcing the status-quo comparative advantage in REE production. In contrast, an REE subsidy shifts the comparative advantage toward home country and has a long-term impact by reducing the global price of REE through trade. Quantitative analysis indicates that the climate provisions of the Inflation Reduction Act increase the US and global welfare by 0.12% and 0.03%, respectively, and reduce global carbon emissions by 1.37% through 2100. Compared to an RE subsidy, an REE subsidy implemented by the US (i) increases the global share of US equipment, (ii) is less efficient in reducing carbon emissions in the US but more effective in reducing global carbon emissions, and (iii) results in higher welfare for both the US and globally, with significant heterogeneity across countries.
  • Learning and Expectations in Dynamic Spatial Economies (Supplementary Appendix) (with Jingting Fan and Fernando Parro) NBER WP #31504
  • Abstract: The impact of shocks in dynamic environments depends on how forward-looking agents anticipate the path of future fundamentals that shape their decisions. We incorporate flexible beliefs about future fundamentals in a general class of dynamic spatial models, allowing beliefs to be evolving, uncertain, and heterogeneous across groups of agents. We show how to implement our methodology to study both ex-ante and ex-post shocks to fundamentals. We apply our method to two settingsâ€”an ex-ante study of the economic impacts of climate change, and an ex-post evaluation of the China productivity shock on the U.S. economy. In both cases, we study the impact of deviations from perfect foresight on different outcomes.
  • The Carbon Cost of Free Trade: Optimal Carbon Tax as a Trade Policy Disguise.
    • Abstract: This paper examines how the optimal carbon tax responds to tariff reduction and explores its implications for carbon emissions. When the import tariff is set below the optimal level, the carbon tax incorporates carbon leakage and terms of trade motives. Both of these motives lead to a lower optimal carbon tax rate compared to the case under the optimal tariff. Quantitative exercises suggest that while the direct impact of tariff reduction on carbon emission is ambiguous and small, the indirect impact, through lowering the optimal carbon tax, is sizable and exacerbates global carbon emission.
